Emerald Greenway Boston

Why Emerald Greenway is an Urban Transformation

  • Aims to address mobility, quality of life, economic development, climate resilience, equity and opportunity, and liveability.

  • Compliments existing transport systems in Boston by providing a walkable alternative to motorised transport. Proposed routes will run alongside major thoroughfares as a more environmentally conscious option for commuters.

  • The city-wide project is also a revival of Boston's previous legacy of interconnected city parks. Parks and greenways have existing in Boston since its foundation in 1630, but over time some of the land has been repurposed into industrial and residential zones. This project aims to restore the greenways beyond their original state.

Describing the Transformation

  • The Emerald Network's "greenways" are 200 miles of shared-use paths linking Boston's neighbourhoods to the city.

  • Existing popular walkways are being redeveloped into four types of greenways - park paths, off-road paths, neighbourways, and greenway connectors.

  • Key areas of development include revamped lighting, safer street crossings, wayfinding terminals, more trees, and connections to parks and plazas.

Ways to measure success

Social Media Analysis

Social media analysis is always a good way to collect the opinions, and also locations, of people who mention Boston's Emerald Network. Platforms like Twitter, Facebook, and Instagram have location tagging and location-specific hashtags which can help narrow down the results to accounts are in, or have been using the Networks greenways.

Arguments in favor and against the Transformation

  • Boston's City Council identified that 42% of the ~5000 people who took part in the Boston 2030 survey said they support the Greenways project.

  • In reality, development has been slow and construction so close to roadways has restricted access to houses and businesses (Perille, 2018).

  • On the other hand, the currently completed network fully encircles the CBD and makes it easier for commuters to walk to and from the inner neighbourhoods without crossing streets.
